Module 3

Digitalization, AI & Data in HR

People Analytics GenAI Ethics

Data and AI are the enablement layer of new-age HRM: they make people decisions faster, more evidence-based, and more scalable. The senior discipline is capturing that upside without importing bias or eroding trust.

From reporting to insight to foresight

  • Interpreting data for actionable insight — moving past dashboards to decisions: what should we actually do differently?
  • Behavioral modeling & predictive analytics — anticipating attrition, performance, and skills gaps before they become crises.
  • GenAI across the lifecycle — applications in HR strategy (planning, recruitment, selection) and in learning, performance, and career management.
  • Learning experience platforms (LXPs) — personalized, continuous development at scale.

Ethics is not an appendix

Every capability above has a paired risk. Predictive models trained on past decisions can automate historical bias at scale. GenAI in selection raises questions of transparency, explainability, and candidate consent. Analytics on employee data raises privacy and surveillance concerns. The ethical issues in the digitalization of HRM are therefore a core design constraint, not a compliance afterthought — the credibility of the entire data agenda depends on getting them right.

Scenario

An HR team deploys a GenAI tool to screen and rank applicants, trained on a decade of the company's own hiring decisions. Time-to-screen drops sharply and managers are delighted. Three months later, an audit shows the tool systematically down-ranks candidates from non-traditional backgrounds — mirroring a bias that was present in the historical data.

The lens: the productivity win was real, but unexamined training data encoded an old bias and scaled it. The fix is not to abandon the tool but to govern it — audit for disparate impact, keep humans accountable for decisions, and demand explainability before deployment.

Refresh your knowledge?

People analytics is the use of workforce data to inform decisions; predictive analytics extends this to anticipating future outcomes such as attrition or skills gaps.

GenAI (generative AI) can produce text, summaries, and recommendations — useful across recruitment, learning, and performance, but requiring oversight for accuracy, bias, and transparency.

Algorithmic bias occurs when a model trained on historical data reproduces and scales the biases embedded in that data, even without explicit intent.
